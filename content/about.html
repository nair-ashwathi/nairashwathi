Namaste! I am a final-year PhD researcher at the Swinburne University of Technology. I am also part of the ARC Centre of Excellence for Gravitational Wave Discovery (OzGrav), in Melbourne, Australia. I sstudy the formation channels of gravitational wave (GW) sources by predicting the properties of mergers from diverse astrophysical scenarios. I would love to dive deeper into each source's (by each, I mean binary black hole, black hole-neutron star (BHNS), binary neutron star) formation pathways and the physical processes that give them their unique properties. But my PhD thesis focuses on the formation of double neutron stars and black hole-neutron star binaries. Talk about detection via radio telescopes. Detections by LIGO \citep[][]{2015CQGra..32g4001L} and Virgo \citep[][]{2015CQGra..32b4001A} have so far identified two double neutron star (DNS) mergers. The first DNS, which merged ... was detected in 2017, which was nicknamed GW170817 (all astrophysists' favourite DNS binary!), which had component masses and total mass ... very much like the ones we observed in the radio wavelength in our Galactic field. However, the second detection, which took place in 2019, the source now nicknamed GW190425 had component masses of the neutron stars in the mass range of the observed ones, it had a total mass of $3.4^{+0.3}_{-0.1}$\,$\mathrm{M_{\odot}}$ which is more massive than any other observed DNS. This hints at the uncertainty in the formation of such heavy DNS systems. After the detection, many different formation pathways have been studied. My role in this drama is to test all the formation channels and identify which of those is most likely to give birth to a GW190425-like heavy system! I use the Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA), a 1D stellar evolution code, to compare theoretical predictions with DNS population observed in radio and GWs, thereby enhancing our understanding of their progenitors and underlying physical processes.
